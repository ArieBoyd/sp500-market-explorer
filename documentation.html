01 · Design Process

How the Visualization Was Designed

The design process began with a guiding question: "What can the structure of the S&P 500 tell us about how sectors and companies compare in terms of size, growth, and market performance?" From that central question, the design unfolded in three phases.

Phase 1

Question First, Data Second

Before touching any code, the goal was to identify what questions a finance-minded user would actually want to answer. Questions like: How has the overall market trended over 10 years? Which sectors dominate the index? Do high-growth companies command higher prices? These questions directly determined which chart types were needed.

Phase 2

Sketching Initial Layouts

Initial sketches explored several layout approaches. An early idea was a single large treemap showing all 502 companies by market cap. While visually striking, it made comparison across sectors difficult. A second sketch explored a dashboard grid with smaller coordinated views — this became the final direction because it allowed multiple questions to be answered simultaneously without overwhelming the user.

Phase 3

Iterative Coding & Refinement

Once the grid layout was chosen, each chart was built and tested independently. The scatter plot went through two iterations — the first used a log scale for price which compressed the data too much. Switching to a linear scale with outlier filtering (removing prices above $2,000) produced a much more readable result. The sector bar chart was originally vertical but was changed to horizontal to accommodate the long sector names without rotation.

Phase 4

Linking the Views

The final and most important design step was making the charts talk to each other. Clicking a sector bar now filters the scatter plot, and the sector dropdown stays in sync. This cross-filtering interaction transforms four separate charts into one unified exploratory tool.

02 · Design Rationale

Why Every Choice Was Made

Each visual encoding in this project was a deliberate decision grounded in visualization principles. Below is a rigorous justification for the key design choices.

🎨

Color Encodes Sector

Each of the 11 sectors is assigned a distinct hue from a carefully chosen palette. Color is used exclusively for sector membership — never for any other variable — so the encoding is unambiguous. The dark background maximizes color contrast, making sector membership immediately legible even in the dense scatter plot.

●

Bubble Size = Market Cap

In the scatter plot, bubble radius is encoded using a square-root scale (d3.scaleSqrt) so that the visual area of each bubble is proportional to market cap — not the radius. This prevents large-cap companies like AAPL from appearing disproportionately large and distorting the comparison.

📈

Line Chart for Time Series

The S&P 500 index data is continuous over time, making a line chart the most appropriate encoding. An area fill beneath the line was added to reinforce the direction of change (up = filled, meaningful). The 1Y/3Y/5Y/All toggle allows users to zoom into recessions or bull markets without losing the full picture.

↔

Horizontal Bar Chart for Sectors

A horizontal layout was chosen for the sector chart because sector names are long strings that would require rotation in a vertical bar chart — a known readability issue. Horizontal bars allow labels to be read naturally left-to-right, and bars are sorted by count (descending) to support rapid comparison.

🌑

Dark Theme

A dark background was chosen deliberately for two reasons: it matches the aesthetic of professional financial terminals (Bloomberg, trading platforms), which signals domain literacy to finance recruiters; and it provides superior contrast for the colored data elements compared to a white background.

🔗

Linked / Coordinated Views

The sector bar and scatter plot are linked: clicking a sector bar filters the scatter plot to that sector. This cross-filtering interaction reduces cognitive load — instead of asking users to mentally track sector membership across two separate charts, they can highlight one sector and instantly see how its companies cluster on the revenue growth vs. price axes.

⚡

Animated Transitions

All bars animate on load using d3.transition() with an ease-cubic-out curve. The line chart uses the stroke-dashoffset animation technique to draw the path from left to right. These animations are not decorative — they guide the user's eye to newly rendered data and signal that the chart has updated in response to an interaction.

📐

2×2 Grid Layout

The four charts are arranged in a 2-column grid, with the line chart and market cap ranking spanning the full width (they are the most "macro" views), while the sector bar and scatter plot share a row (they are the most "company-level" views). This hierarchy mirrors how an analyst would think: market first, then sector, then company.

03 · Findings

What the Visualization Revealed

After building the visualization, it was used to explore the dataset and answer the guiding questions. Below are the key findings, each supported by a screenshot.

Finding 01

The COVID-19 crash of March 2020 is the most dramatic single event in 10 years of S&P 500 history

Using the line chart with the "All" time range selected, the index drop from ~$3,380 to ~$2,305 in just 23 trading days in February–March 2020 is visually unmistakable — a near-vertical drop followed by one of the fastest recoveries in market history, reaching new all-time highs by August 2020.

Finding 02

Technology dominates the S&P 500 with 82 companies — nearly 4× more than Energy or Basic Materials

The sector bar chart makes this imbalance immediately visible. Technology (82), Industrials (70), and Financial Services (67) together account for nearly half of all 502 index constituents. Energy and Basic Materials each have only 22 companies despite representing critical sectors of the real economy, suggesting the index heavily favors growth-oriented sectors.

Finding 03

High revenue growth does not reliably predict high stock price — but extreme outliers cluster in Technology

Filtering the scatter plot to Technology sector reveals a cluster of high-growth, high-price companies (NVDA, AAPL, MSFT) but also shows many technology companies with moderate growth and low prices. The large bubble sizes in this cluster confirm that market cap — not growth rate alone — is what separates mega-caps from the rest of the sector.

Finding 04

AAPL, NVDA, and MSFT together represent a market cap larger than most entire sectors combined

The Top 15 Market Cap chart makes this striking fact concrete: AAPL alone at $3.8T exceeds the combined market cap of all 22 Energy companies in the index. The top 3 Technology companies (AAPL, NVDA, MSFT) represent approximately $10.3T in market cap — dwarfing sectors like Real Estate, Utilities, and Basic Materials entirely.

04 · Dataset

Data Sources & Attributes

This project uses two CSV files from the S&P 500 Stocks (Daily Updated) dataset published on Kaggle by Andrew Mvd. Together they provide 9 attributes across two files — within the required 5–10 attribute range.

Attribute File Type Used In
Date sp500_index.csv Temporal Line chart X-axis
S&P500 sp500_index.csv Quantitative Line chart Y-axis
Symbol sp500_companies.csv Categorical Scatter tooltip, market cap chart labels
Sector sp500_companies.csv Categorical Color encoding, sector bar chart, filter
Currentprice sp500_companies.csv Quantitative Scatter Y-axis
Marketcap sp500_companies.csv Quantitative Bubble size (scatter), market cap bar chart
Revenuegrowth sp500_companies.csv Quantitative Scatter X-axis
Ebitda sp500_companies.csv Quantitative Tooltip detail
Weight sp500_companies.csv Quantitative Tooltip detail

The dataset was sourced from kaggle.com/datasets/andrewmvd/sp-500-stocks. No external cleaning scripts were used — all data parsing and filtering was handled in-browser using D3's built-in CSV loader with row accessor functions.
